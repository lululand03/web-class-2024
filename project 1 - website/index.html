Working Girl

“To sell art is to share a piece of oneself, but to sell sex can feel like a betrayal of that self—a tension that defines so much of my experience.”


On the author

Sophia Giovannitti is a multifaceted artist and writer known for her exploration of the intersections between art, labor, and identity. Through her diverse body of work, she examines the complexities of contemporary existence, blending personal narrative with broader social commentary. Giovannitti’s artistic practice includes various mediums such as photography, performance, and installation, each piece reflecting her engagement with themes of commodification, vulnerability, and empowerment. Her work often challenges traditional perceptions of femininity and labor, as she prompts audiences to reconsider the value placed on both art and the bodies that create it.

On "Working Girl"

In her book Working Girl: On Selling Art and Selling Sex, Giovannitti explores the often-overlapping realms of the art world and sex work. The narrative intertwines her personal experiences with a critical analysis of societal norms surrounding femininity, labor, and the economics of both art and sex.
Through a candid exploration of her own journey, she highlights the ways in which women navigate these spaces, negotiating their identities and desires within frameworks that often seek to objectify or commodify them. The book serves as a commentary on the intricate dynamics of power, autonomy, and the artistic spirit in a world that often conflates value with visibility.

Author's positions "on selling art and selling sex"

Sophia Giovannitti's thoughts on art, sex, and capitalism revolve around the intricate relationships between these elements, highlighting how they intersect and influence one another in contemporary society. She critiques the commodification of both art and sex, arguing that capitalism often reduces complex human experiences and identities to mere transactions. This perspective emphasizes the ways in which women, in particular, navigate spaces where their bodies and artistic expressions are subjected to market forces.
Giovannitti views the art world as a microcosm of broader societal issues, where the value of art is frequently determined by its commercial viability rather than its intrinsic meaning or emotional resonance. She explores how the expectations placed on women—both as artists and as individuals—often lead to a duality in their roles, forcing them to balance personal authenticity with societal demands. By examining the commodification of femininity, she illuminates the challenges women face in asserting their agency within these frameworks, suggesting that reclaiming one's narrative is an act of resistance against the objectification inherent in both capitalism and traditional gender roles.
Giovannitti's work calls for a reevaluation of the value systems that govern art and sex, advocating for a deeper understanding of the personal and political implications of selling oneself in a capitalist society. She encourages readers to consider how these dynamics shape not only individual experiences but also collective identities and movements for empowerment.
